The bad news and the good news

THE BAD NEWS

Let me start by saying I don't have a problem with something just because it's new. I'm not scared of new technologies. If you visited my home office you would find many things that look like they came out of a science fiction movie. There are microcontrollers plugged into different sensors, spare chips, LCD screens reporting data back from some of those sensors, a Windows and Mac computers, several Linux servers amongst other things.

However, I do know people that are afraid of new technologies and the future, and rightly so when they are bombarded with headlines like the ones above. I don't have a grudge against "AI" because it took my job or because it might. In fact, the opposite is true: I'm an AI engineer, and my job is to deploy AI ethically and safely (number one) and where it makes sense, which are use cases supported by research (number two). So in my case business is booming and my own job prospects don't concern me.

Replacing labour with a cheaper alternative sits at the top of many CEOs' wish lists. That's at the scale of companies. But more broadly, the world is partly governed by money-driven primates (which is technically correct). There are several historical examples of this being the case, but probably the quickest way to verify if there is some truth to what I'm saying is to look behind the device you are reading this on. If it's an iPhone, it probably says "made in China." A Samsung phone was likely made in Vietnam or India. A Dell, HP, or Lenovo PC was probably also made in China.

In the example above, the existing workforce at the time was replaced with cheaper labour in countries with lax rules and weak protections for human rights or care for the environment. Right now, the ambition is to replace human labour with algorithms, in a broad sense, and more specifically with the transformer architecture introduced by Google in the 2017 paper Attention Is All You Need.

It is a sad fact, but it is true. Many people will choose shareholder primacy, in other words, greater profits in the next quarter over the well-being of their workforce, real people with families, dreams, and hopes. Because let's be clear: when you choose to replace somebody with an algorithm, as we are being told is now happening with this technology, you are looking away from the lives of the people affected by it, all in the name of maximising a number in a spreadsheet.

This can't be hidden behind "progress", the "race against China", or the "inevitability of disruption". Those are the comfortable words of people that don't mind pulling the ladder up behind them. Every algorithm deployed in place of a person it's a choice made in a boardroom, blessed by charts, and then handed down as something inevitable. But when you strip everything back and cut through the language of "disruption" and "efficiency", what you are left with is a simple truth: we built tools capable of cool things, and the first thing we asked them to do was make certain people irrelevant

THE GOOD NEWS

Right now, most people being replaced by "AI" are not being replaced because some science-fiction AI can actually do their job. Replacing someone with something is not a testament to the capability of whatever is being used as the replacement. Proof of capability is passing a capability test and actually being able to do the task. A company might decide tomorrow to replace all their workers with cats. That wouldn't mean the cats could do the job. It would probably be a better indication of the CEO's mental health. Today, for the most part, a company that replaces part of its workforce with AI (and this is well documented) is really saying: our finances aren't going well, we need to let people go, so let's put out a press release saying "we are replacing 10% of the workforce with AI," because that makes us look like a forward-thinking business instead of admitting we are struggling financially and had to let many people go.

As it stands today, we are lucky enough that the technology doesn't live up to the promise. The "PhD-level" AI (according to Sam Altman, OpenAI) and the "country of geniuses living in a data center" (Dario Amodei, Anthropic) scores less than 1% on ARC-AGI-3. ARC-AGI-3 is a set of easy puzzles that most humans would score 100% on. So where the state-of-the-art models from OpenAI and Anthropic, powered by extremely expensive computers and enormous amounts of electricity, effectively score 0% once rounded (!), an average person like me, powered by a cup of coffee (I had no breakfast that morning), scores 100% without breaking a sweat and barely using my brain. That's me attempting the puzzles under the same conditions the LLM attempted them under: I hadn't seen them before, and no one explained to me how to solve them. Bear in mind that when I say a lot of electricity, I mean that. Using the best model from OpenAI and Anthropic had a cost of almost $10,000 (USD) each, and they used enough electricity to power an average household for a couple of days.

Humans are still far cheaper and more efficient than AI. At a fraction of the cost we can solve a puzzle that these systems, with a much greater budget, can't. This benchmark in particular, ARC-AGI-3, sets an action limit: the AI is only allowed to take a certain number of steps per level, specifically no more than 5 times the number of moves a typical human needs to complete it. This is done so companies don't game the puzzle by simply brute-forcing the solution (attempting thousands of times, with a budget that would surpass several million dollars, until the puzzle is solved). So ARC-AGI-3 tests whether these systems can do something that is easy for us humans, which is discovering rules autonomously and the ability to update beliefs as new evidence appears.

So what is the good news, you might ask?

The good news is that, although these systems are impressive tools and are, no doubt, useful for many things, they aren't what they are being advertised as so we still have time. Time to have an honest conversation about what this technology is and what it isn't. Time to legislate so that the workers don't have to fear being replaced by those who put technology before people. Time to demand transparency from companies that market statistical prediction calculators as digital gods or living things.

And perhaps most importantly, we still have time to remember the value of people. A society where a teacher, artist, software developer, tradesperson, or customer support worker is discarded the moment a cheaper approximation appears is a society that has forgotten its own purpose. Progress should only be measured by how much we improve everyone's lives.
